Isolation and Abandonment

The boat is described as old, broken, and stranded far from the water, symbolizing the characters' own sense of isolation and abandonment. Like the boat, the clones at Hailsham are cut off from the wider world and left to decay, forgotten by society. The boat's desolate state mirrors the clones' fate—they are created for a specific purpose (organ donation) and discarded once they have served that purpose.

Lost Dreams and Unattainable Freedom

The boat also symbolizes the characters' longing for freedom and a life beyond their predetermined roles. For Kathy, Tommy, and Ruth, the boat becomes a place of imagination and escape, where they dream of a future that is ultimately unattainable. It represents their desire to break free from their fate, but its stranded and broken condition underscores the futility of their hopes.

Mortality and Decay

The boat's decayed state reflects the inevitability of the clones' mortality. Just as the boat is slowly eroding and falling apart, the clones are destined to "complete" (die) after donating their organs. The boat serves as a haunting reminder of their limited lifespan and the inescapable nature of their existence.

Memory and Nostalgia

For Kathy, the boat becomes a poignant memory of her childhood and her relationships with Tommy and Ruth. It symbolizes the fleeting nature of happiness and the bittersweet quality of nostalgia. The boat is a place where they once felt a sense of wonder and possibility, but it also reminds them of how much they have lost over time. And after this experience, Ruth seemed to wake up, she sincerely apologized to Kathy, and wanted Kathy and Tommy to stay together, it may be because she really woke up, or it may be because she felt that she would not survive the donation, so she wanted to do something for the two friends before she left.

The Outside World – A Society That Ignores Them

The clones live on the margins of society, rarely interacting with normal humans. The outside world benefits from their existence but refuses to acknowledge them. Madame’s Reaction: When the students see Madame outside of Hailsham, she instinctively recoils from them—showing that even those who advocate for them still see them as other. The Ethical Blindness of Society: Society has justified the clones' existence for medical purposes, but they are not given any real rights or recognition. The Clones’ Lack of Resistance: Despite their mistreatment, the clones never attempt to rebel or escape, showing how deeply they have been conditioned to accept their role. Symbolism and Significance: The outside world represents willful ignorance and moral detachment. It is a society that benefits from injustice while avoiding confrontation with the ethical dilemmas it creates.
